The Companion
Four ways to explore your Twin
Each mode is suited to a different kind of inquiry. Select a mode to see example questions and how to use it.
Forward mode

→ Explore — “What kind of life fits who I am?”

Use Explore when you want to think about your future, your direction, your goals, or the environments where you would thrive. This is the Vacation Planner: instead of asking “where should I go?” it asks “given who I actually am, where would I flourish?”

Explore is teleological — it connects your values to the shape of a life worth living for you specifically. It is not about predicting the future. It is about designing it intentionally.

Example questions to bring to Explore mode
  • “Given who I am, what kind of career would feel meaningful rather than just successful?”
  • “What environment — city, community, kind of organization — fits my moral structure?”
  • “If I had one year to reinvent my direction, what would be worth pursuing?”
  • “What trade-offs am I willing to accept to live according to my values?”
  • “What kind of relationships, practices, and work are aligned with who I actually am?”
Counterfactual mode

🔬 Experiment — “What if things had been different?”

Experiment mode is where the app becomes genuinely playful and profound. It treats your identity as path-dependent and asks: which parts of you are essential, and which are contingent on the specific circumstances of your actual history?

This is identity simulation, ethical counterfactuals, and narrative rewriting — philosophy meets alternate timelines. The insight it produces is usually about what remains constant across the alternate versions, not about the differences.

Example questions to bring to Experiment mode
  • “If I had pursued a different field, what kind of person would I be now?”
  • “What if I had grown up in a different culture — how would my values differ?”
  • “If I removed fear of failure from my decision-making, what would I have done differently?”
  • “What choice from my past most shaped who I am now — and what did it cost?”
  • “What remains constant about me across all these alternate timelines?”
Present mode

💬 Reflect — “Help me think through something real.”

Reflect is the best starting point for most students, and the mode most directly connected to SAGE reasoning. Bring something real from your life right now: a decision you are navigating, a tension you are living with, a conflict, a reaction that surprised you.

The key difference from talking to a friend or a generic AI: the Companion engages from within your specific moral framework. You feel “seen structurally” — your reasoning is reflected back to you with precision, not generic wisdom.

Example questions to bring to Reflect mode
  • “I have two options and I don’t know which one to choose — help me think this through.”
  • “Why did I react so strongly to this situation? What does that reveal?”
  • “What values are actually in conflict here, and how do I weigh them?”
  • “I feel like I’m being asked to compromise something important — what is it?”
  • “What would I advise a friend in my situation — and why can’t I take my own advice?”
Restoration mode

☕ Recover — “I’m not ready to think deeply yet.”

Recovery Mode is one of the most human-centered parts of the app. Instead of forcing reflection when you are depleted, it begins from a different premise: sometimes the most philosophical act is not analysis, but restoration.

Recovery Mode identifies what kind of burden you are carrying (body, time, relationship, structure, or identity) and offers specific, low-cost actions at whatever depth feels possible — from a one-minute reset to a full reflective conversation.

Important: Low coherence is not failure. It is information about what you need. If you feel overwhelmed, exhausted, or like everything is too much — Recovery Mode first, then return to examination when you have capacity.
What to bring to Recovery Mode
  • “I’m overwhelmed and I don’t know where to start.”
  • “Everything feels urgent and nothing is getting done.”
  • “I don’t feel like myself right now and I’m not sure why.”
  • “I need to slow down before I can think clearly.”
The Coherence Check-In
Six domains, one slider each, thirty seconds. Physical energy · Temporal horizon · Relational coherence · Structural clarity · Identity integrity · Recovery access.

Don’t focus on any single reading. Instead ask: what patterns do I see over time? When do I feel most clear? What situations consistently drain me? The trajectory is the data.
Course sequence
Seven assignments + final project
The assignments follow the formal trajectory of identity development: build the model, apply it, test it against counterfactuals, expand its moral horizon, examine the conditions that make ethical judgment possible. Click any assignment to expand it.
All graded work is based on reflection, not disclosure. You are never required to share personal or sensitive experiences. The journals ask what you noticed, what surprised you, what questions the app opened — not the specific content of your conversations.
1
Mapping My Moral Architecture
Weeks 1–2 · 600–800 words

Complete the PRF22 and at least four Assembly History responses. Read your generated profile slowly.

Journal prompt: Where does your profile feel precisely right — and where does it feel slightly off or missing? Identify one value that strongly defines you, one tension or contradiction in your profile, and one life experience that shaped your moral perspective. Then: how does your moral “architecture” influence how you see ethical problems?

Learning goal: Students begin to understand they are not neutral observers — ethical reasoning is structured by identity and history.

Also in week 2: Write your Letter to Future Self. You will not read it until week 14.

ProfilePRF22Assembly History
2
The Living Ethical Problem
Weeks 3–4 · 600–800 words

Open Companion → Reflect mode. Bring a real issue: a personal dilemma, a conflict, a difficult decision. Have at least 6–8 message exchanges. Save the exchange to Archive.

Journal prompt (SAGE):
S — Seeing: What is actually happening?
A — Analyzing: Who are the stakeholders? What values are in tension?
G — Generating: What possible responses exist?
E — Engaging: What would you actually do — and why?

Required: How did the Companion help you see something you had not previously noticed?

Stuck after a few exchanges? Tell the Companion: “I feel like we’ve covered this — what am I not seeing yet?” That question almost always opens a new direction.

3
Counterfactual Self
Weeks 5–6 · 600–800 words

Open Companion → Experiment mode. Explore one alternate version of yourself: a different major, a different culture, a self without fear of failure.

Journal prompt: Describe the alternate version of yourself. How would this version make different ethical decisions? What values would change? What remains constant? Final question: what does this reveal about which parts of your identity are essential versus contingent?

Learning goal: Identity is constructed. Ethics is path-dependent. Self-understanding enables flexibility.

Experiment modeIdentityCounterfactuals
4
Designing My Future
Weeks 7–8 · 600–800 words

Open Companion → Explore mode. Ask: “Where would I flourish given who I am?” and “What kind of life fits my moral structure?” Explore career paths, environments, and lifestyles.

Journal prompt: Describe a life that is ethically aligned with who you are. Include: environment, relationships, work, practices. Key question: what trade-offs must you accept to live this life?

Learning goal: Connect ethics to life design. Understand constraints and trade-offs. Think teleologically — ends and means together.

Explore modeLife designTeleological ethics
5
Coherence and Breakdown
Weeks 9–10 · 400–600 words

Complete 3–5 Coherence Check-ins over a week. Observe patterns across domains.

Journal prompt: What patterns do you notice in your coherence across time? What increases coherence? What decreases it? Which domain is most fragile? Core reflection: how does your ability to make good ethical decisions change when your coherence is low?

Learning goal: Ethics depends on conditions of agency, not just on reasoning. This is the course’s central empirical claim.

Coherence check-inATCFAgency conditions
6
Recovery Before Judgment
Weeks 10–11 · 400–600 words

Use Recovery Mode at least once. Identify your burden type, choose recovery actions, save the Recovery Note.

Journal prompt: Describe a moment when you were not capable of clear ethical thinking. What was your burden? What did recovery require? How did your thinking change afterward? Critical question: should ethical responsibility account for a person’s level of coherence?

Learning goal: Ethical judgment must consider human limits. Repair requires recovery before it can succeed.

Recovery modeCare ethicsMoral injury
7
The Archive as Identity
Weeks 12–13 · 600–800 words

Review your saved conversations, reflections, and recovery notes. Identify patterns across the whole semester.

Journal prompt: What story is your archive telling about you? Include: recurring concerns, growth points, unresolved tensions. Final question: who are you becoming?

Learning goal: See identity as dynamic. Recognize ethical development over time as a process, not an event.

ArchiveNarrative ethicsLongitudinal identity
∞
Final Project — My Personal Ethical Framework
Weeks 14–15 · 1200–1500 words

Use your archive as your evidence base. This is not a conventional essay summarizing course concepts. It is an argument about your own development as an ethical agent, supported by what you have actually recorded across the semester.

Final question: How will you continue living an examined life beyond this course?

Include: your PRF and how it shaped your reasoning this semester · one application of SAGE to a significant issue · a reflection on coherence and the conditions for your best ethical thinking · who you are becoming and what you intend to do about it.

Read your Letter to Future Self (written in week 2) before writing this project.

Data and disclosure
Privacy guidelines for this course
Your data belongs to you. This page explains exactly what the app stores, what is transmitted, and what you are and are not required to share.

What stays in your browser

  • Your philosophical profile
  • All six assembly history essays
  • Your complete Companion archive
  • All coherence check-in readings
  • Recovery notes and history

What is transmitted when you use the Companion

  • Each message — encrypted via HTTPS/TLS
  • A summary of your profile context
  • Processed by Anthropic’s Claude API
  • Anthropic does not train on API data
  • Subject to Anthropic’s privacy policy

You are NOT required to

  • Share personal or sensitive experiences
  • Reveal private information
  • Submit raw app data as graded work
  • Use your full name within the app

You should NOT

  • Upload Twin backup files to Canvas
  • Share another student’s information
  • Access someone else’s data
  • Store exported files carelessly
If you are in crisis: The Examined is not a mental health service. If you are experiencing thoughts of harming yourself or others, or feel unable to care for yourself, please call or text 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline, 24/7) or contact a mental health professional. Do not continue with the app right now.
Always use the app from the course URL — not from a downloaded file. Your data is stored in the browser at the origin where you first used the app. Opening a different file or a different browser means a different storage location. Bookmark: rmertzman-tech.github.io/The-Examined/

Getting started
Your first session in five steps
Complete these steps in your first class session. They take about fifteen to twenty minutes.
1
Open the app
Go to the course URL
Open rmertzman-tech.github.io/The-Examined/ in your browser. Bookmark it. Always use this URL — not a downloaded file. Read the first-visit disclosure and click “I understand — begin.”
2
PRF22 assessment
Twenty-two scenario questions — take your time
There are no right answers. These questions map the structure of your moral attention — what you notice first, what you weight most heavily. Answer from instinct, not from what you think you should say.
3
Assembly History
Six questions about your life — be specific
The depth of your answers here directly determines the precision of your profile. A few sentences per question produces a sketch. Several paragraphs produces something you will recognize. You can always return to deepen your answers later.
4
Read your profile
Slowly. Notice what fits and what doesn’t.
The places where the profile misses you are as important as the places where it is right. Both are philosophical territory. The profile is a first approximation, not a final word. You will revise it over the course of the semester.
5
Write your Letter to Future Self
200 words. You will not read it until week 14.
In the Archive tab, find Letter to Future Self. Write to yourself as you are right now. What do you hope you don’t forget? What are you trying to become? What should pull you back if you drift off track? Save it and do not read it again until the final week.
Tips for getting the most from it
How to use the Companion well
💡

Bring something real

The Companion works best with actual situations from your life — not hypotheticals or abstract questions.

💬

When stuck, say so

Tell the Companion: “I feel like we’ve covered this — what am I not seeing yet?” That question almost always opens a new direction.

💾

Save what matters

After an exchange where something clicked, save it. The archive is your most important instrument for the final project.

📷

Deepen your answers

Return to your Assembly History and add more detail when you notice the profile missing something important about you.

📈

Check in at inflection points

Use the coherence check-in when something changes — not on a fixed schedule. The pattern matters, not the frequency.

☕

Recover before examining

When you are depleted, Recovery Mode first. You cannot examine yourself honestly when you are running on empty.

One question to start with if you don’t know where to begin:
“Given who I am right now, what is one thing I should understand about myself that I am not seeing clearly?”
